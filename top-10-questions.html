Top 10 / Cross-examination

Questions the record cannot answer.

These are not rhetorical flourishes. They are pressure tests. Each question forces the institution to choose between uncertainty as clinical nuance and certainty as administrative fact.

  1. If final, why uncertain?
    The discharge artefact cannot both preserve unresolved diagnostic caution and perform settled closure without explaining the bridge.
  2. If uncertain, why certified?
    Certification is not a mood. It creates reliance.
  3. If separate, why responsive?
    A symptom treated as outside the frame cannot be casually reabsorbed when medication response becomes useful.
  4. If responsive, why peripheral?
    Dopaminergic or centrally mediated response makes the foot signal neurologically material until excluded.
  5. If Parkinsonian, why podiatry?
    Podiatry may support surface care. It cannot answer neurological causation.
  6. If podiatric, why Sinemet?
    A podiatric explanation does not account for the later repeated recognition of Parkinson-medication effect.
  7. If open, why closed?
    Openness must be visible in the acts, not only in the prose.
  8. If closed, why still returning to the feet?
    The longitudinal notes keep rediscovering the same signal the closure displaced.
  9. If reviewed, why not revised?
    Review of the patient is not review of the premise.
  10. If no one could know, why certify as if they knew?
    This is the cleanest contradiction: uncertainty may excuse not knowing; it does not excuse acting with certainty.
The institution cannot rely on uncertainty as clinical nuance and certainty as administrative fact.

Top 10 / Neutralisation

Things the record made non-governing.

The danger was not simple omission. The record often saw the fact. It then denied the fact authority over the conclusion.

  1. The foot-centred entry problem.
    The case entered through gait, pain, function, and feet. Finality proceeded without neurologically resolving that signal.
  2. The dystonic-looking gait.
    Dystonic language appeared, but dystonia did not become the governing explanation.
  3. The footdrop-like movement that was not foot drop.
    A non-peripheral-looking sign was recorded, then not allowed to pull the feet back into neurology with sufficient force.
  4. The patient's youth.
    Young onset widened the differential and increased the stakes, but ordinary finality still arrived.
  5. The atypical presentation.
    Atypicality was described as narrative texture rather than treated as a brake on closure.
  6. The possible genetic disease.
    PRKN and monogenic possibility were recognised, then operationally postponed.
  7. The deferred levodopa discriminator.
    The drug was treated as therapeutically delicate, but its diagnostic consequence was not allowed to limit certainty.
  8. The possibility of future regret.
    Foreseeable regret was written into the document that nevertheless closed the case.
  9. The non-movement-disorder disclaimer.
    The limitation was acknowledged but produced no visible ownership shift.
  10. The bedside protest.
    The patient restored the original problem in ordinary language. The closing record did not preserve it.
A red flag only functions as a red flag if it changes conduct.

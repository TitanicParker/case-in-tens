Essay 3

The Feet Were Not Incidental

On the GP referral, foot-centred entry, gait, and podiatry displacement.

FeetReferralPodiatry
The case entered through the feet. The record closed over them.

Thesis

The feet were not incidental because they defined the accepted scope of inquiry. The hospital was not merely asked to classify a neurological label. It accepted a problem of walking, function, pain, posture, and foot-centred abnormality.

Once accepted, that problem had to be resolved, falsified, or explicitly superseded before finality could safely attach.

Support versus displacement

Podiatry may have been appropriate as support. Surface pathology can require surface care. But supportive podiatry is not the same as neurological explanation. The core problem is that the feet appear to drift outward without the record adequately explaining why they no longer governed the diagnostic question.

That drift matters because a surface feature can also be downstream evidence of central motor dysfunction. The foot may be the page on which the movement disorder writes itself.

The continuity marker

The feet remain the continuity marker across the case. They are present at entry, in the discharge narrative, in the protest, in later neurology notes, in medication-response discussions, and in later crisis points. A fact that returns this often is not incidental.

The question for the record is therefore simple: where, before discharge and certification, was the foot-centred problem resolved?
